The innermost layer of a blood vessel wall	Tunica interna (intima)	Made of endothelium plus a thin layer of connective tissue. The endothelium acts as a selective barrier, releases dilation/constriction signals, and normally keeps platelets from sticking — damage here is what starts clot formation.	2
The middle layer of a blood vessel wall	Tunica media	Mostly smooth muscle and elastic fibers. This is the layer that actually changes vessel diameter (vasoconstriction/dilation) and resists blood pressure.	2
The outermost layer of a blood vessel wall	Tunica externa (adventitia)	Loose connective tissue that anchors the vessel and carries small nerves and the vasa vasorum into the wall.	3
Small vessels that supply blood to the walls of large arteries and veins themselves	Vasa vasorum	Needed because the vessel wall is too thick for the blood inside it to nourish by diffusion alone.	3
The three size classes of arteries, largest to smallest	Conducting (elastic), distributing (muscular), resistance arteries	Conducting arteries (aorta, carotids) stretch and recoil to smooth out pressure surges; distributing arteries route blood to specific organs; resistance arteries/arterioles are the main site of flow control.	4
Why conducting arteries have so much elastic tissue	They expand during systole and recoil during diastole to smooth out pressure surges	This protects smaller downstream vessels from the full force of each heartbeat. Age-related stiffening of this tissue (arteriosclerosis) is why blood pressure tends to rise with age.	4
Smallest arteries, the major point of control over blood flow into a tissue	Arterioles	Only 1-3 layers of smooth muscle. Because they're so numerous and muscular relative to their size, arterioles account for about half of the body's total peripheral resistance.	5
Three types of capillaries	Continuous, fenestrated, sinusoid	Continuous = tight walls (muscle, brain, lungs); fenestrated = pores for fast filtration (kidneys, intestine, endocrine glands); sinusoids = large gaps, used where whole cells/proteins need to cross (liver, spleen, bone marrow).	6
Capillary type with pores that allow rapid filtration of small molecules	Fenestrated capillaries	Found in places doing rapid absorption or filtration: kidneys, small intestine, choroid plexus, endocrine glands.	6
Capillary type with wide gaps and no basal lamina, allowing whole cells and proteins to pass	Sinusoids	Found in liver, bone marrow, spleen. This is how new blood cells enter circulation and how the liver releases plasma proteins like albumin.	6
Cells that wrap around some capillaries and may help regulate blood flow	Pericytes	Also can differentiate into endothelial or smooth muscle cells, contributing to vessel repair and growth.	6
A short vessel that lets blood bypass a capillary bed, connecting an arteriole directly to a venule	Metarteriole (thoroughfare channel)	Common in places like the mesentery; lets the body redirect flow around resting capillary beds.	7
The muscle cuff that regulates blood flow into an individual capillary	Precapillary sphincter	Not directly innervated by nerves — responds only to local chemical signals and hormones, not the autonomic nervous system.	8
Why veins are called "capacitance vessels"	They're thin-walled and expand easily, holding most of the body's blood volume at rest	At rest, roughly 64% of blood volume sits in the systemic veins vs. only about 15% in systemic arteries.	8
Structures inside medium/large veins that keep blood moving toward the heart	Venous valves	Prevent backflow under gravity. Their failure (from prolonged standing, pregnancy, obesity) leads to varicose veins.	9
A blood route where blood passes through two capillary beds in series before returning to the heart	Portal system	Examples: hypothalamus-to-pituitary, intestines-to-liver (hepatic portal system), and the kidneys.	10
A point where two blood vessels other than capillaries connect	Anastomosis	Arterial anastomoses give collateral routes of supply (important around joints); venous anastomoses give alternate drainage routes, so blocking one vein is rarely as dangerous as blocking one artery.	10
A shunt where blood flows directly from an artery into a vein, bypassing capillaries	Arteriovenous anastomosis	Found in fingers, palms, toes, ears — helps limit heat loss in the cold, but also makes these areas more prone to frostbite.	10
The formula relating blood flow, pressure, and resistance	Flow is proportional to the pressure difference divided by resistance (F ∝ ΔP/R)	Bigger pressure gradient = more flow; more resistance = less flow. This is the core equation behind everything in hemodynamics.	11
Difference between systolic and diastolic pressure	Pulse pressure	For a BP of 120/75, pulse pressure = 45 mm Hg. It reflects the force/stress that pressure surges put on the arteries.	12
A close estimate of the average pressure driving blood through the body over a full cardiac cycle	Mean arterial pressure (MAP) ≈ diastolic pressure + one-third of pulse pressure	For 120/75, MAP ≈ 75 + 15 = 90 mm Hg. MAP (not systolic/diastolic alone) is what most affects risk of things like atherosclerosis, aneurysm, and stroke.	12
The three variables that determine peripheral resistance to blood flow	Blood viscosity, vessel length, vessel radius	Of these three, only vessel radius changes significantly minute to minute (via vasoconstriction/dilation) — viscosity and length are essentially fixed in the short term.	13
How much flow changes when a vessel's radius doubles	Flow increases 16-fold (flow is proportional to radius to the 4th power)	This is why arterioles are such a powerful lever for controlling blood flow — small changes in diameter produce huge changes in flow.	14
The brain region that exerts sympathetic control over blood vessels throughout the body	Vasomotor center (medulla oblongata)	Integrates baroreflexes, chemoreflexes, and the medullary ischemic reflex to adjust vessel diameter and blood pressure.	15
A negative feedback reflex that responds to changes in blood pressure, detected in the carotid sinuses	Baroreflex	Rising BP increases baroreceptor firing, which inhibits sympathetic tone and dilates vessels to bring BP back down. Important for short-term adjustments like standing up quickly.	15
A reflex that responds to blood pH, O2, and CO2 levels, detected in the carotid and aortic bodies	Chemoreflex	Low O2, high CO2, or low pH trigger widespread vasoconstriction and increased breathing to restore normal blood gases.	16
The ability of a tissue to regulate its own blood supply based on local conditions	Autoregulation	Low oxygen and metabolite buildup (CO2, H+, lactate, adenosine) trigger local vasodilation until perfusion catches up with demand.	15
An increase in blood flow above normal after a period of reduced flow to a tissue	Reactive hyperemia	Classic example: skin flushing/reddening after coming in from the cold, once circulation resumes.	15
The growth of new blood vessels in a chronically hypoxic tissue	Angiogenesis	Important in uterine lining regrowth, athletic training adaptations, and unfortunately in tumor growth (which is why some cancer drugs target angiogenesis).	15
Only about this percent of total blood volume is inside capillaries at any moment	5% (250-300 mL)	Even though it's a small fraction, this is the blood actually doing the work of exchange with tissues.	17
The two forces that determine whether fluid leaves or enters a capillary	Hydrostatic pressure (pushes fluid out) and colloid osmotic pressure (pulls fluid in)	Net filtration pressure is generally outward at the arterial end of a capillary and reverses to net reabsorption at the venous end.	19
Why capillaries filter fluid out at the arterial end but reabsorb it at the venous end	Blood hydrostatic pressure drops substantially along the capillary's length while osmotic pressure stays about the same	At the arterial end, hydrostatic pressure dominates (net filtration); by the venous end, it has dropped enough that osmotic pressure dominates (net reabsorption).	19
What happens to fluid that capillaries filter out but don't reabsorb	It's picked up by the lymphatic vessels and returned to the blood	Capillaries typically reabsorb about 85% of what they filter; the rest becomes lymph.	20
Accumulation of excess fluid in a tissue	Edema	Three basic causes: increased capillary filtration, reduced capillary reabsorption (e.g., low blood albumin), or blocked lymphatic drainage.	20
Why low blood albumin (hypoproteinemia) causes edema	It lowers the blood's colloid osmotic pressure, so less fluid gets pulled back into the capillaries	Common in liver disease (albumin is made in the liver), severe burns, and protein malnutrition.	21
The five mechanisms that help return venous blood to the heart	Pressure gradient, gravity, skeletal muscle pump, thoracic (respiratory) pump, cardiac suction	Since venous pressure is low, the body relies on several passive/active mechanisms working together rather than pressure alone.	22
How the skeletal muscle pump helps venous return	Contracting muscles squeeze veins, pushing blood toward the heart; venous valves stop it from flowing backward	This is why staying still for long periods (long flights, prolonged standing) promotes venous pooling in the legs.	22
How breathing assists venous return	Inhaling drops pressure in the thoracic cavity and raises pressure in the abdominal cavity, squeezing blood in the inferior vena cava toward the heart	Called the thoracic (respiratory) pump; valves prevent the blood from being pushed back down into the legs.	22
Pooling of blood in the lower body from prolonged standing or sitting	Venous pooling	Can reduce cardiac output enough to cause dizziness or fainting if the skeletal muscle pump isn't active (e.g. standing rigidly at attention).	22
Any state where cardiac output is insufficient to meet the body's metabolic needs	Circulatory shock	Falls into two broad categories: cardiogenic shock (heart pumping problem) and low venous return (LVR) shock (not enough blood getting back to the heart).	22
Shock caused by loss of blood volume (hemorrhage, dehydration, burns)	Hypovolemic shock	The most common form of low venous return shock.	22
Shock caused by a sudden loss of vasomotor tone, letting vessels dilate widely	Neurogenic shock	A type of venous pooling shock; can result from brainstem trauma or a strong emotional/pain response.	23
Shock involving both vasodilation and increased capillary permeability from bacterial toxins	Septic shock	Combines features of hypovolemic shock (fluid loss through leaky capillaries) and venous pooling shock (vasodilation).	23
Difference between compensated and decompensated shock	Compensated shock triggers reflexes (baroreflex, angiotensin II) that restore blood pressure on their own; decompensated shock involves positive feedback loops that worsen without treatment	About half of people who progress to decompensated shock die from it, since ischemia and clotting further damage the heart and brain in a self-reinforcing cycle.	23
Why cerebral blood flow stays remarkably stable even when systemic blood pressure changes	The brain autoregulates: cerebral arteries dilate when systemic BP drops and constrict when it rises	This keeps flow steady across a MAP range of roughly 60-140 mm Hg; below 60 causes fainting, above 160 can cause cerebral edema.	23
Temporary episode of dizziness, vision loss, weakness, or other neurological symptoms from brief cerebral ischemia	Transient ischemic attack (TIA)	Often an early warning sign of an impending stroke; requires prompt evaluation.	24
Why skeletal muscle blood flow can increase more than 20-fold during exercise	Local metabolites (lactate, CO2, H+, adenosine, nitric oxide) trigger arteriole dilation in active muscle	This requires diverting blood away from organs like the kidneys and digestive tract, which is why heavy exercise right after a big meal can cause GI discomfort.	24
Why pulmonary arteries have lower blood pressure than systemic arteries	They have thinner, less elastic walls suited for a low-resistance circuit	Pulmonary BP is only about 25/10 mm Hg. This lets blood move slowly enough through the lungs for efficient gas exchange, and keeps osmotic pressure dominant so capillaries there absorb fluid rather than leak it into the alveoli.	24
How pulmonary arterioles respond to local hypoxia, compared to systemic arterioles	Pulmonary arterioles constrict in low oxygen, while systemic arterioles dilate	This redirects blood away from poorly ventilated lung regions toward better-ventilated ones — the opposite strategy from the rest of the body, but it makes sense for matching blood flow to air flow.	24
